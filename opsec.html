üîí Extreme Anonymity - OpSec Guide

Operational Security (OpSec) is the practice of protecting critical information that could be used against you. In the digital world, this means understanding what data you're exposing and how to minimize your digital footprint.

What is OpSec?

OpSec (Operational Security) is a process that identifies critical information and implements measures to protect it from adversaries. It's about controlling what information is available about you and preventing it from being used to identify, locate, or harm you.

Key Principle: Anonymity is about people knowing what you're doing but not knowing who you are. Privacy is about people knowing who you are but not knowing what you're doing.

  • IP Address & Network Tracking Your IP address reveals your approximate location and ISP. Use VPNs, Tor, or proxies to mask your real IP. Never access sensitive accounts from your home network.
  • Device Fingerprinting Browsers and websites can identify you through unique combinations of your browser settings, screen resolution, installed fonts, and hardware specs. Use Tor Browser or hardened Firefox to minimize fingerprinting.
  • Metadata Leaks Files, photos, and documents contain hidden metadata (GPS coordinates, device info, timestamps, author names). Always strip metadata before sharing anything online.
  • DNS Leaks Your DNS requests can reveal which websites you visit even when using a VPN. Use encrypted DNS (DoH/DoT) or configure your VPN to handle all DNS requests.
  • Operating System Telemetry Windows, macOS, iOS, and Android constantly send usage data to their parent companies. Use Linux distributions focused on privacy (Tails, Whonix, QubesOS) for sensitive operations.
  • Phone Number & Email Verification Services that require phone verification can link your identity to your account. Use anonymous phone numbers (Hushed, MySudo) or services that don't require phone verification.
  • Behavioral Patterns Your writing style, posting times, interests, and online behavior can identify you. Vary your patterns, use different personas, and avoid linking accounts through similar behaviors.
  • Compartmentalization Never mix your real identity with anonymous personas. Use separate devices, accounts, and networks for different identities. Create clear boundaries between your online personas.
  • Use Tor for Anonymity Tor Browser routes your traffic through multiple nodes, making it extremely difficult to trace. Use Tor for all anonymous activities. Never log into real accounts through Tor.
  • Burner Email Addresses Create disposable email addresses using ProtonMail, Tutanota, or temporary email services. Never reuse emails between different personas or activities.
  • Cryptocurrency Privacy Bitcoin transactions are traceable. Use Monero (XMR) for truly private transactions. If using Bitcoin, use mixers/tumblers, but understand they're not foolproof.
  • Secure Operating Systems Use Tails (amnesia OS that leaves no trace), Whonix (Tor-focused OS), or QubesOS (security through compartmentalization). Avoid Windows and macOS for sensitive work.
  • Full Disk Encryption Encrypt your entire drive with strong passphrases. Use VeraCrypt or LUKS. Consider plausible deniability with hidden volumes.
  • Secure Communication Use Signal for encrypted messaging. Use PGP for encrypted email. Never trust proprietary encryption. Verify encryption keys through separate channels.
  • Physical Security Destroy HDDs physically (drill holes through platters). Securely wipe SSDs with multiple passes. Use privacy screens. Be aware of cameras and surveillance.
  • Tails OS Live operating system that leaves no trace. Routes all traffic through Tor. Runs from USB. Perfect for maximum anonymity.
  • Whonix Two-VM setup that forces all traffic through Tor. Protects against malware and IP leaks. More secure than using Tor Browser alone.
  • QubesOS Security-focused OS using virtual machine compartmentalization. Isolates different activities. Advanced but extremely secure.
  • VeraCrypt Open-source disk encryption. Create hidden volumes for plausible deniability. Successor to TrueCrypt.
  • MAT2 (Metadata Anonymization Toolkit) Removes metadata from files. Essential before sharing documents, images, or any files online.
  • KeePassXC Offline password manager. Generate strong unique passwords. Never store passwords in cloud services.
  • OnionShare Share files anonymously through Tor. No third-party servers. Perfect for sensitive file transfers.
  • Virtualbox/KVM Run virtual machines to compartmentalize activities. Test suspicious files in isolated VMs. Create disposable environments.
  • Tor over VPN Connect to VPN first, then Tor. Hides Tor usage from ISP. VPN can't see your traffic (Tor handles that). Adds extra layer.
  • Multiple Proxy Chains Route traffic through multiple proxies/VPNs in different jurisdictions. Makes traffic analysis extremely difficult.
  • MAC Address Randomization Change your MAC address regularly. Use macchanger on Linux. Prevents tracking via hardware identifiers.
  • Plausible Deniability Create hidden encrypted volumes within encrypted volumes. If forced to reveal password, reveal the outer volume only.
  • Anti-Forensics Use RAM-only operating systems (Tails). Securely wipe files with multiple passes. Encrypt swap partitions. Disable logs.
  • Air-Gapped Systems Use computers never connected to internet for critical operations. Transfer data via USB with strict protocols.
  • Stylometry Avoidance Alter your writing style. Use tools to analyze your writing patterns. Avoid unique phrases or expressions.
  • Time Zone Obfuscation Set your system to UTC. Don't reveal your timezone through posting patterns. Be aware of metadata timestamps.
  • ‚ùå Mixing Identities Never access both real and anonymous accounts from the same device, network, or session. One mistake can link everything.
  • ‚ùå Reusing Usernames/Emails Using the same username across platforms makes you easy to track. Create unique identities for each purpose.
  • ‚ùå Trusting Free VPNs Free VPNs often log data, inject ads, or sell your information. Use reputable paid VPNs or Tor for anonymity.
  • ‚ùå Ignoring Metadata Photos contain GPS coordinates, camera model, timestamps. Documents contain author names and edit history. Always strip metadata.
  • ‚ùå Using Windows/macOS for Sensitive Work These operating systems have extensive telemetry and are closed-source. Use Linux-based privacy-focused OSes instead.
  • ‚ùå Relying Only on Tor Browser Tor Browser protects browsing but your OS might leak data. Use Tails or Whonix for complete anonymity.
  • ‚ùå Social Engineering Vulnerability Don't reveal personal details even in anonymous contexts. People can piece together information from multiple sources.
  • ‚ùå Not Having a Burn Plan Know how to quickly destroy evidence if compromised. Have encrypted backups. Plan emergency procedures.
